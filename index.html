Redlining deepened the rift between the dreams and achievable goals.

1921-
1940

When everyone chases the American Dream too far, you end up with the Great Depression. Everyone desperately scrambled to the top of the capitalist machine via the Stock market, and it inevitably crashed. The Great Depression was the antithesis of the preceding years, known as the gilded age. During the Great Depression, there was rampant homelessness, and Bank failure meant many people lost their life savings. President Franklin Delano Roosevelt's solution to this homelessness started the process of redlining through the National Housing Act of 1934. In "Homelessness and Black History: Access to Housing," the NAEH notes that the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) made through Residential Security Maps. Green areas meant the best area, where the "best" people (businessmen) lived; blue areas meant "good" (white-collar workers) people, while red meant "hazardous" areas, where primarily foreign immigrants, low-class whites, and people of color lived. Redlining made it challenging for residents to buy or refinance a house, receive public service of any kind, or own houses of significant property value. In the NAEH's words, "The federal government began implementing new programs designed to make housing more accessible … these programs … excluded Black Americans" (NAEH). The structure of the quote highlights just how sinister this form of discrimination was. The author describes the program in the way the government would: a beneficial program for all. The immediate switch-up reflects the double-sidedness of these programs and how the US government sneakily used the program to undermine Black wealth accumulation. Merrefield explains this further, stating, "black families faced dual barriers to wealth accumulation: they paid more in rent for similar housing while the homes they were able to purchase rapidly declined in value" (Merrefield). The government weaponized the Great Depression to gate-keep the "American Dream" from Black people by only serving the portion of Americans it deemed “worthy." These issues persist in "A Raisin in the Sun" because redlining put Black families like the Youngers in positions where they have to fight for their dreams against government institutions that hold them back, where the systemic issues and inequalities that factored into the American Dream were not even their fault. The Youngers being able to buy a house was an exception, one that was met with hostility from their white neighbors.
